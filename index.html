Brazilian Jiu Jitsu

What is Bjj?

Brazilian Jiu Jitsu is a grappling martial art that is predominantly ground-based. It is descended from Judo, which itself is descended from traditional Japanese Jiu-Jitsu. More recently freestyle wrestling and greco-roman wrestling techniques have also been incorporated. Unlike most traditional martial arts, BJJ prioritises hard-sparring as a training method which ensures all techniques are battle-tested by encountering daily resistance by millions of practioners.

Training Method

The importance of hard-sparring as an aspect of the training method cannot be overstated. It trims the fat and unnecessary fluorishes from techniques, ensuring they are all technically sound and viable against opponents that have a size advantage on you. I would never practice a martial art that didn't incorporate regular hard-sparring rounds as a central aspect of training.

Practitioners often wear a kimono (or a gi) made from durable fabric during training, inherited from judo. A judoka or a jiu jitsu practitioner trains techniques that utilise an opponent's clothing to toss them to the ground or even choke them unconcious. To practice these techniques jumpers and T-shirts would result in the fabric being torn to shreds in minutes running up a huge Primark bill. A kimono is a useful, though not perfect, substitution for these things.

However, in the modern era of jiu jitsu roughly half of your training will be "nogi". This means you'll be wearing shorts and a T-shirt or a rashguard. During nogi grappling no grips can be made on clothing. This is partly to simulate fighting in MMA rules (the founders of BJJ also founded the UFC), and partly to satisfy the demands of the consumer market. Many practioners find the gi overly bulky and hot.
